ERP Chart of Accounts Layer 0

1. Overview

The Chart of Accounts (CoA) is the financial classification backbone of the enterprise. Every monetary transaction ultimately posts to a GL account, and the CoA defines the complete taxonomy of those accounts. It is a Layer 0 block that is independently deployable — it can be loaded as a separate metadata app without requiring other blocks to exist first.

Three Fundamental Questions Answered:
  1. Where is value classified? GL accounts as the atomic posting target.
  2. How are accounts organized? Account groups for reporting structure (Balance Sheet, Income Statement).
  3. What is the account's financial nature? Account type and subtype for automatic debit/credit behavior and financial statement placement.

Because it is dependency-free, the Chart of Accounts can be deployed, validated, and populated before any transactional ERP block exists. Every financial domain — Budgeting, Journal Entries, Accounts Payable, Accounts Receivable — references the CoA.

Compatibility with Event-Driven Accounting

This design is intentionally compatible with the event-driven accounting model planned for Layer 1. In that model, Economic Events (sales, purchases, payments) are the primary transactional concept — journal entries are derived representations, not manually created artifacts. GL accounts serve as classification targets that Economic Events post to, rather than being the source of transactions themselves. This distinction keeps the CoA purely structural: it defines where value is classified, not how it gets there.

8. Design Decision Points

Account Groups vs Account Hierarchy

Decision: Maintain two independent structural mechanisms with distinct purposes.

  • Account hierarchy (erp-account-parent-rel) = computational structure for balance consolidation. Drives reactive computations, balance roll-up, and posting constraints.
  • Account groups (erp-account-group-has-account-rel) = presentational structure for financial statement sections. Can change independently (IFRS vs GAAP migration) without affecting computational roll-up.

Rationale: Regulatory changes, new reporting standards, or management preferences may require regrouping accounts without altering their computational parent. The two assignments are independent.

Aggregation for Group-Account Relationship

Decision: Use aggregation-relator (not composition) for erp-account-group-has-account-rel.

Rationale: When a group is dissolved or reorganized, the underlying GL accounts must survive — they contain historical posting data. Accounts are reassigned to new groups. Composition would imply the account's lifecycle is bound to the group, which is incorrect.

Recursive Account Hierarchy for Consolidation

Decision: Use a self-referencing erp-account-parent-rel (GL Account → GL Account) with aggregation semantics.

Rationale: Enterprise GL systems commonly have 3+ levels of nesting (Assets → Current Assets → Cash). The recursive structure naturally maps to reactive computation cascade at Layer 1. Aggregation is correct because children survive parent restructuring.

Normal Balance Stored Explicitly

Decision: Store normal_balance as an explicit Categorical property (Debit/Credit) rather than deriving it from account type.

Rationale: Contra accounts reverse the normal direction:

  • Accumulated Depreciation — Asset account with Credit normal balance
  • Sales Returns — Revenue account with Debit normal balance
  • Owner's Drawings — Equity account with Debit normal balance

Storing it explicitly avoids special-case logic for contra accounts.

Control Accounts and Subsidiary Ledgers

Decision: Declare is_control_account as a boolean at Layer 0; enforcement at Layer 1.

Rationale: At Layer 0, we declare intent (this account backs a subsidiary ledger). At Layer 1, the posting engine enforces the invariant: control account balance = sum of subsidiary entries. Examples: Accounts Receivable (acct 1120), Accounts Payable (acct 2100).

Account Type vs Account Subtype (Two-Level Classification)

Decision: Separate into 5 fundamental types and 15+ subtypes, each as its own enum universal with attribution relators.

Rationale:

  • Account Type (5 values) determines fundamental behavior: accounting equation side, financial statement (Balance Sheet vs Income Statement), normal balance direction.
  • Account Subtype (15+ values) provides finer classification for regulatory reporting, depreciation schedules (Fixed Assets), aging reports (Current Assets), etc.

Both are attribution relators (not properties) because they reference enum universals with their own identity and potential for future enrichment.

is_summary as Explicit Boolean

Decision: Store is_summary as an explicit boolean property rather than inferring it from the presence of children in the hierarchy.

Rationale: During account restructuring, a leaf account may temporarily become a parent (or vice versa). Inferring summary status from hierarchy presence would create ambiguity during transitions. The explicit boolean makes the posting constraint unambiguous: the Layer 1 posting engine rejects any posting to an account where is_summary = true, regardless of current hierarchy shape.

10. Layer Information & Deployment

Layer 0 — Independently Deployable

The Chart of Accounts block has zero dependencies on other ERP blocks. It can be loaded, validated, and populated before any transactional Layer 1 block exists. The only optional cross-block reference is to Org Structure's erp-cost-center for default cost allocation mapping — this is an association (not a dependency), and the CoA functions fully without it.
